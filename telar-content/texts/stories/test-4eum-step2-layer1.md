**

Chimu culture developed in the dry coastal deserts of Northern Peru and became one of the largest and influential civilizations in the Andes before the expansion of the Inca Empire. Their capital city, Chan Chan, was an enormous adobe urban center containing palaces, storage complexes, temples, and ceremonial spaces. Due to the region receiving very little rainfall, the chimu relied on sophisticated irrigation canlas to support agriculture and sustain a growing population. 
 

 

Chimu society was highly structured and divided social class. Elites and rules controlled labor systems, trade networks, and the production of luxury goods such as fine textiles, metalwork, and ceramics. Artists and craftsmen created detailed ceremonial objects, including whistling vessels, that often reflected religious beliefs and political authority. Religion played a major role in Chimu life, with rituals connected to the ocean, agriculture, ancestors, and supernatural forces. The Chimu were eventually conquered by the Incan Empire, but many of their artistic and technological traditions continued to influence Andean culture thereafter.
